    By Gennady Retseptor

    Were people who lived in Antiquity and Medieval times familiar with the 40 inventive principles of TRIZ? Having studied famous Latin phrases, quotes and proverbs, the answer appears to be positive. The 40 inventive principles, discovered by Genrich Altshuller and his colleagues as patterns for technical problems solution, also represent fundamental and universal principles of human wisdom and creativity.5

    Principle 1. Segmentation (Division)

    A. Divide object or system into independent parts.

    • Divide et impera – "Divide and govern (rule, conquer)" (Philip II of Macedonia). Roman maxim of ruling subdued nations.
    • Gallia est omnis divisa in partes tres – "All of Gaul is divided into three parts" (Caesar).
    • Facilius per partes in cognitionem totius adducimur – "We are more easily led part by part to an understanding of the whole" (Seneca).

    B. Make object or system sectional. Make object or system easy to assemble-disassemble.
    C. Increase the degree of fragmentation or segmentation.

    • De minimis – With respect to trifles.

    D. Transition to micro-level.

    • Natura in minima maxima – Nature is the greatest in the smallest things.

    Principle 2. Taking Out (Separation, Isolation)

    A. Separate an interfering (weak) part or property from object or system.

    • Nulla regula sine exceptione – No rule without exception.
    • Pars pro toto – Part instead of whole. Sacrifice part in order to save whole.
    • Intra muros – Within the walls. Not public.
    • Amoto quaeramus seria ludo – "Joking aside, let us turn to serious matters" (Horace).
    • Absente reo or In absentia – In the absence. A trial carried out in the absence of the accused.
    • Interfice errorem, diligere errantem – "Kill the sin, love the sinner" (St. Augustine).

    B. Single out the only necessary (and sufficient) part or property of object or system.

    • Cura nihil aliud nisi ut valeas – "Take care of nothing except that you do well" (Cicero).
    • Dictum sapienti sat est – "A word to the wise is sufficient" (Terence).
    • Conditio sine qua non – Condition without which not. Indispensable condition.

    Principle 3. Local Quality (Non-uniformity)

    A. Change object or system structure from uniform to non-uniform. Use object or system non-uniformity.
    B. Change an external environment (or external influence) from uniform to non-uniform. Use external non-uniformity.
    C. Make each part of object or system function in conditions most suitable for its operation.
    D. Make each part of object or system fulfill a different and/or complementary useful function.

    • Ad hoc – Toward this. For the special purpose. Something created or improvised especially for a particular occasion.
    • Punctum saliens – The outstanding point. The essential or most notable point.
    • Rem acu tetigisti – "You have hit the nail on the head" (Plautus). You have touched the matter with a needle. You have described it accurately.
    • Multum in parvo – Much in little.
    • Non multa, sed multum – "Not many things, but much" (Quintilian). Not quantity, but quality.
    • E pluribus unum – "From many, one" (Virgil).
    • Primus inter pares – "First among equals" (A title of the Roman emperors).
    • Multi sunt vocati, pauci vero electi – Many are called, but a few chosen (Vulgate Bible).
    • Non licet omnibus adire Corinthum – "Not everybody is granted privilege of going to Corinth" (Horace).
    • Cui bono? – "Whom does it benefit?" A maxim of Cassius, quoted by Cicero. Used in the detection of crime.
    • Cui prodest scelus, is fecit – "The crime was committed by somebody who benefits from it" (Seneca).
    • Suum quique – To each what he deserves.
    • Reddite ergo quae sunt Caesaris, Caesari – "Give Caesar what's Caesar's" (Vulgate Bible).
    • Vos vestros servate, meos mihi linquite mores – "You keep to your own ways and leave mine to me" (Petrarch).
    • De gustibus non est disputandum – Matters of taste are not to be disputed.
    • Trahit sua quemque voluptas – Each man is led by his own taste.
    • Sua cuique voluptas – Everyone has his own pleasures.
    • Quot capita, tot sententiae or Quot homines, tot sententiae – "As many opinions as people" (Terence).
    • Libenter homines id quod volunt credunt – "People gladly believe what they want to" (Caesar).
    • Nullus est instar domus – There is no place like home.
    • Magna res est vocis et silentii temperamentum – "The great thing is to know when to speak and when to keep quiet" (Seneca).
    • Sutor, ne ultra crepidam – "Cobbler, no further than the sandal" (Apelles). It is said that Greek painter Apelles was one day painting a warrior but he was uncertain on how to render his sandals (crepida). He asked the advice of a cobbler (sutor), but after a time the cobbler started offering advice on other parts of the painting and was rebuked by Apelles with this phrase.

    Principle 3 Inverted. Global Quality (Uniformity)

    • Unus multorum – One of many. An average person.
    • Amicus omnibus, amicus nemini or Multus amicus, nullius amicus – A friend to all is a friend to none.

    Principle 4. Asymmetry (Symmetry Change)

    A. Change the shape of object or system from symmetrical to asymmetrical.
    B. Change the shape or properties of object or system to suit external asymmetries.
    C. If object or system is asymmetrical, increase its degree of asymmetry.

    • Audaces fortuna iuvat – "Fortune favors the brave" (Virgil).
    • Fortes fortuna iuvat – "Fortune favors the strong" (Terence).
    • Vae victis! – "Woe to the conquered!" (Livy).
    • In dubio pro reo – "When in doubt, in favour of the accused" (Corpus Juris Civilis).
    • Satius est impunitum relinqui facinus nocentis, quam innocentem damnari – "It is better that a crime is left unpunished than that an innocent man is punished" (Corpus Juris Civilis).
    • Inter dominum et servum nulla amicitia – No friendship between master and servant.
    • Quod licet Jovi, non licet bovi – "All that is allowed to Jupiter is not allowed to an ox" (Terence). What the privileged can do, the rest cannot.
    • Quod medicina aliis, aliis est acre venenum – One person's medicine is another's foul poison.
    • In alio pediculum, in te ricinum non vides – "You see a louse on someone else, but not a tick on yourself" (Petronius).
    • Qui dedit benificium taceat; narret qui accepit – "Let him who has done a good deed be silent; let him who has received it tell it" (Seneca).

    Principle 4 Inverted. Symmetry (Reciprocity)

    • Do ut des – Give to obtain. Principle of reciprocity.
    • Quid pro quo – A thing for a thing. A reciprocal exchange, something given in compensation.
    • Qui vult dare parva non debet magna rogare – He who wishes to give little shouldn't ask for much.
    • Manus manum lavat – "One hand washes the other" (Petronius). A favor for a favor.
    • Serva me, servabo te – "Save me and I will save you" (Petronius).
    • Lex talionis – Law of retaliation. Retributive justice. An eye for an eye.
    • Audi et alteram partem – Hear the other side.
    • Audiatur et altera pars – Hear both sides.

    Principle 5. Merging (Combining, Integration, Harmony)

    A. Bring closer together (or merge) identical or similar objects or systems; assemble identical or similar parts to perform parallel operations.

    • Adde parvum parvo manus acervus erit – "Add little to little and there will be a big pile" (Ovid).
    • Concordia res parvae crescent – Work together to accomplish more.
    • Coniunctis viribus – With united powers.
    • Viribus unitis – By united efforts.
    • Consensu omnium – By general consent. By the agreement of all.
    • Oculi plus vident quam oculus – Several eyes see more than only one.

    B. Make operations contiguous or parallel; bring them together in time.

    • Pari passu – With equal pace. Moving together, simultaneously.

    C. Agglomerate objects or systems to bi- and poly-system.

    • Concordia civium murus urbium – Harmony of citizens is the wall of cities.
    • Concordia salus – Well-being through harmony.
    • Ubi concordia, ibi victoria – Where there is a harmony, there is a victory.

    Principle 6. Universality (Multi-functionality)

    A. Make a part of object or system perform multiple functions; eliminate the need for other parts.

    • Ab uno disce omnes – "Learn all from one thing" (Virgil).

    B. Use standardized features.

    • Pecuniate obediunt omnia – All things obey money.
    • Nervos belli, pecuniam – "The nerve of war, money" (Cicero).
    • Nihil tam munitum quod non expugnari pecunia possit – "No fort is so strong that it cannot be taken with money" (Cicero).
    • Pecunia non olet – "The money does not smell" (Vespasian). About the plan to tax public urinals.

    Principle 6 Inverted. Non-universality

    • Ne Jupiter quidem omnibus placet – Not even Jupiter can please everyone.
    • Nec scire fas est omnia – We are not allowed to know all things.
    • Non omnia possumus omnes – "All of us cannot do everything" (Virgil).

    Principle 7. Nesting

    A. Place one object or system inside another; place each object, in turn, inside the other.

    • Imperium in imperio – An empire within an empire. A fifth column, a group of people within a nation's territory who owe allegiance to some other leader.
    • Ars est celare artem – "It is art to conceal art" (Ovid).
    • Obscurum per obscurius – The obscure by means of the more obscure.

    B. Make one part pass (dynamically) through a cavity in the other.

    • Deux ex machina – A god from a machine. Sudden, unexpected turnover. A contrived event that resolves a problem at the last moment.

    Principle 8. Anti-Weight (Seeking Help from Higher Forces)

    A. To compensate for the weight of object or system, merge it with other objects or systems that provide lift.
    B. To compensate for the weight of object or system, make it interact with the environment (e.g., use buoyancy and other forces).

    • Leve fit, quod bene fertur, onus – "A burden which is borne well becomes light" (Ovid).
    • Auxilio ab alto – By help from on high.
    • Deo adjuvante, non timendum – With the help of God there is nothing to be afraid of.
    • Deux vult – "God wills it!" (A motto of the Crusades).
    • Nil sine numini – Nothing without Providence.
    • Annuit coeptis – God has favored us.
    • Cito fit quod dii volunt – "What the Gods want happens soon" (Petronius).
    • Nam homo proponit, sed Deus disponit – "Man foretells, God decides" (Thomas A.Kempis).
    • Fortuna suffragante – With fortune on our side.
    • Avito viret honore – He flourishes on the honors of his ancestors.
    • Experto credite – "Trust the expert" (Virgil). Believe one who speaks from experience.
    • Ipse dixit – He, himself, has spoken. Used with reference to the arbitrary assertions of authorities.
    • Iurare in verba magistri – Swear by teacher's words.
    • Laudari a viro laudato – To be praised by a man that is praised (i.e., by an eminent man).

    Principle 9. Preliminary Anti-Action (Preventive Measures)

    A. If it will be necessary to do an action with both harmful and useful effects, this action should be replaced with anti-actions to control harmful effects.
    B. Create beforehand stresses in object or system that will oppose known undesirable working stresses later on.

    • Praemonitus, praemunitus – Forewarned, forearmed.
    • Praevenire melius est quam praeveniri – It is better to precede than to be preceded.
    • Ad utrumque paratus – Prepared to the worst.
    • In omnia paratus – Prepared for all things.
    • Semper paratus – Always prepared.
    • Animis opibusque parati – Prepared in minds and resources.
    • Non semper erit aestas – It will not always be summer. Be prepared for hard times.
    • Qui non est hodie cras minus aptus erit – "He who is not prepared today will be less so tomorrow" (Ovid).
    • Adversus incendia excubias nocturnas vigilesque commentus est – Against the danger of fires, he (Augustus) conceived the idea of night guards and watchmen.
    • Principiis obsta – Resist the beginnings. Undesirable trends should be nipped in the bud.
    • Venienti occurrite morbo – Meet the coming disease. Take disease in time, prevention is better than cure.

    Principle 10. Preliminary Action

    A. Perform, before it is needed, the required change of object or system (either fully or partially).

    • Aspirat primo Fortuna labori – "Fortune smiles upon our first effort" (Virgil).
    • Dimidium facti qui coepit habet. Sapere aude, incipe! – "He who has begun has half done. Dare to be wise; begin!" (Horace).
    • Omne initium est difficile – Every beginning is difficult.
    • Primordia cuncta pavida sunt – All beginnings are frightful.
    • Omnium rerum principia parva sunt – "Everything has a small beginning" (Cicero).
    • Ex nihilo nihil fit – "Nothing comes from nothing" (Lucretius). The Conservation Law in philosophy and modern science.
    • Bene diagnoscitur, bene curatur – Something that is well diagnosed can be cured well.
    • Ut sementem feceris ita metes – "As you sow so will you reap" (Cicero).
    • Prior tempore, prior iure – First in time, first by right. First come, first served.

    B. Pre-arrange objects or systems such that they can come into action from the most convenient place and without losing time for their delivery.

    Principle 10 Inverted. Afterward Action

    • Exitus acta probat – "The result justifies the deed" (Ovid).
    • Finis coronat opus – "The end crowns the work" (Ovid).
    • Ex post facto – From after the fact (e.g., a law with retroactive effect).
    • Post hoc, ergo propter hoc – "After this, therefore because of this" (A logical fallacy).
    • Mali principii malus finis – The bad end of a bad beginning.
    • Male parta male dilabuntur – "What has been wrongly gained is wrongly lost" (Cicero). Ill-gotten gains seldom prosper.
    • Post factum nulum consilium – After the deed, no advice is helpful.
    • Serum est post facta consilium – The advice after the deed is too late.
    • Nemo me impune lacessit – "No one provokes me with impunity" (Motto of the Kings of Scotland).
    • Culpam poena premit comes – "Punishment follows close behind crime as its companion" (Horace).
    • Pede poena claudo – "Punishment comes limping" (Horace). Retribution comes slowly, but surely.
    • Nulla avarita sine poena est – "There is no avarice without penalty" (Seneca).

    Principle 11. Beforehand Cushioning (Caution, Alertness)

    A. Prepare emergency means beforehand to compensate for the relatively low reliability of object or system.

    • Abundans cautela non nocet – Abundant caution does no harm.
    • Primum non nocere – "First, do no harm" (Oath of Hippocrates).
    • Quidquid agis, prudenter agas et respice finem – Whatever you do, do cautiously, and look to the end.
    • Cave quid dicis, quando, et cui – Beware what you say, when and to whom.
    • Fide, sed qui, vide – Trust but take care whom.
    • Ne fronti crede – Do not trust to appearances.
    • Mus uni non fidit antro – "A mouse does not rely on just one hole" (Plautus).
    • In cauda venenum – In the tail (of the scorpion) is the poison. Look beyond the obvious in considering possible danger.
    • Lates anguis in herba – A snake is hidden in the grass" (Virgil). Beware of a treacherous person, a false friend, a hidden danger or an unknown risk.
    • Struit insidias lacrimis cum femina plorat – "When a woman weeps, she is setting traps with her tears" (Dionysius Cato).
    • Caveat emptor – Let the buyer beware.
    • Caveat venditor – Let the seller beware.
    • Sed quis custodiet ipsos custodies? – "But who will guard the guardians themselves?" (Juvenal).

    Principle 12. Equipotentiality (Equality)

    A. In a potential field, limit position changes (e.g., change operating conditions to eliminate the need to raise or lower objects in a gravity field).

    • Omnia mors aequat – Death levels all distinctions.
    • Pallida mors aequo pulsat pede pauperum tabernas regumque turres – "Pale Death, with impartial foot, knocks at the cottages of the poor and the palaces of kings" (Horace).
    • Omnes una manet nox – "The same night awaits us all" (Horace).
    • Ignorantia legis neminem excusat – Ignorance of the law excuses no one.
    • Iustitia omnibus – Justice for all.
    • Patria est communis omnium parens – "The fatherland is the common parent of us all" (Cicero).
    • Sol lucet omnibus – "The sun shines for everyone" (Petronius).
    • Sub sole nihil novi est – "There is nothing new under the sun" (Vulgate Bible).
    • Nemo propheta in patria sua – "No one is considered a prophet in his homeland" (Vulgate Bible).
    • Nullum est iam dictum quod non dictum sit prius – "Nothing is said that hasn't been said before" (Terence).
    • Nullum saeculum magnis ingeniis clausum est – "No generation is closed to great talents" (Seneca).
    • Non mihi, non tibi, sed nobis – Not for you, not for me, but for us. The foundation of a good relationship.

    Principle 13. Inversion (The Other Way Round)

    A. Invert the action(s) used to solve the problem (e.g., instead of cooling an object, heat it).
    B. Make movable parts (or the external environment) fixed, and fixed parts movable.
    C. Turn object or system "upside down."

    • Exceptio probat regulam – The exception proves the rule.
    • Aliena nobis, nostra plus aliis placent – "Other people's things are more pleasing to us, and ours to other people" (Publilius Syrus).
    • Quid rides? Mutato nomine de te fabula narratur – "What are you laughing at? Just change the name and the joke's on you" (Horace).
    • Certum est quia impossibile – "It is certain because it is impossible" (Tertullian).
    • Credo quia absurdum – "I believe it because it is absurd" (Tertullian).
    • Omnia iam fient fieri quae posse negabam – "Everything which I said could not happen, will happen now" (Ovid).
    • Homines, dum docent, discunt – "People learn while they teach" (Seneca). Docendo discimus We learn by teaching.
    • Fas est et ab hoste doceri – "It's proper to learn even from an enemy" (Ovid).
    • Legum servi sumus ut liberi esse possimus – "We are slaves of the law so that we may be able to be free" (Cicero).
    • Cui peccare licet peccat minus – "One who is allowed to sin, sins less" (Ovid).
    • Dum excusare credis, accusas – "When you believe you are excusing yourself, you are accusing yourself" (St. Jerome).
    • Excusatio non petita, accusatio manifesta – He who excuses himself, accuses himself.
    • Magna civitas, magna solitudo – A great city is a great solitude.
    • Numquam se minus solum quam cum solus esset – "You are never so little alone as when you are alone" (Cicero).
    • Non ut edam vivo, sed ut vivam edo – "I don't live to eat, but I eat to live" (Quintilian).
    • Non vestimentum virum ornat, sed vir vestimentum – Not the raiment graces the man, but the man the raiment.
    • O praeclarum custodem ovium lupum! – "An excellent protector of sheep, the wolf!" (Cicero).
    • Scio me nihil scire – "I know that I know nothing" (Socrates). True knowledge exists in knowing that you know nothing. And in knowing that you know nothing, that makes you the smartest of all.
    • Stultus puerque vera dicunt – A foolish child speaks truth.
    • Tacent, satis laudant – "Their silence is enough praise" (Terence).
    • Cum tacent, clamant – "When they remain silent, they cry out" (Cicero). Their silence speaks louder than words.
    • Una salus victis, nullam sperare salutem – "The only safety for the defeated is to relinquish all hope of safety" (Virgil).
    • Video meliora proboque deteriora sequor – "I see the better way and approve it, but I follow the worse way" (Ovid).
    • Rerum concordia discors – "The discordant harmony of things" (Horace).
    • Reductio ad absurdum – Reduction to absurdity. Proving the truth of a proposition by proving a falsity of all its alternatives. A technique of argument that proves the thesis by showing that its opposite is absurd or logically untenable. This is an oft-used method of proof in mathematics and philosophy.
    • Armis Exposcere Pacem – "They demanded peace by force of arms" (An inscription on medals).
    • Solitudinem fecerunt, pacem appelunt – "They made a desert and called it peace" (Tacitus).
    • In pace, ut sapiens, aptarit idonea bello – In peace, like a wise man, he appropriately prepares for war.
    • Si vis pacem, para bellum or Qui desiderat pacem, praeparet bellum – "If you want peace, prepare for war" (Vegetius). Peace is least threatened, when the state is prepared for defense.

    Principle 14. Spheroidality (Perfection, Excellence)

    A. Instead of using rectilinear parts, surfaces, or forms, use curvilinear ones; move from flat surfaces to spherical ones; from parts shaped as a cube (parallel-piped) to ball-shaped structures.
    B. Use rollers, balls, spirals, domes.
    C. Go from linear to rotary motion (or vise versa).
    D. Use centrifugal forces.

    • Circulus vitiosus – A vicious circle. Circulus in probando A circle in the proof. The fallacy of using the conclusion as one of the premises.
    • Ad unguem factus homo – "A man polished to the nail" (Horace). A highly polished, accomplished man.
    • Teres atque rotundus – A man polished and complete.

    Principle 14 Inverted. Imperfection

    • Nemo sine vitio est – "No one is born without fault" (Horace).
    • Nemo mortalium omnibus boris sapit – No man is wise at all time. The wisest may make mistakes.
    • Cuiusvis hominis est errare – "Every human can err" (Cicero).
    • Nullum magnum ingenium sine mixtura dementiae fuit – "There has never been great genius without a little madness mixed in" (Seneca).
    • Interdum dormitat bonus Homerus – "Sometimes even the good Homer slumbers" (Horace). Even the best of us makes mistakes.

    Principle 15. Dynamics (Optimization)

    A. Allow (or design) the characteristics of object, external environment, process or system to change to be optimal or to find an optimal operating condition.
    B. Divide object or system into parts capable of movement relative to each other.
    C. If an object or system is rigid or inflexible, make it movable or adaptive.
    D. Increase degree of free motion.

    • Mutatis mutandis – The necessary changes having been made.
    • Tempora mutantur, et nos mutamur in illis – "The times are changed, and we are changed with them" (Ovid).
    • Variatio delectat – "There's nothing like change!" (Cicero).
    • O tempora, O mores! – "Oh, the times! Oh, the morals!" (Cicero).
    • Aurea mediocritas – "The golden mean" (Horace). An ethical goal; truth and goodness are generally to be found in the middle.
    • Est modus in rebus – "There is a middle course in all things" (Horace).
    • In medio stat virtus – "Virtue stands in the middle" (Horace). Virtue is in the moderate, not the extreme position.
    • In medio tutissimus ibis – "In the middle you will go the most safe" (Ovid). Moderation in all things.
    • De duobus malis, minus est semper eligendum – Of two evils, the lesser must always be chosen.
    • Ex malis eligere minima – Choose minimal of two evils.
    • Feci quod potui, faciant meliora potentes – I did what I could; who can – do it better.
    • In necessariis unitas, in dubiis libertas, in omnibus caritas – "In necessary things unity, in doubtful things liberty, in all things charity" (St. Augustine).
    • Malum consilium quod mutari non potest – "It is a bad plan that cannot be changed" (Publilius Syrus).
    • Non progrede est regrede – Not to proceed is to recede. Not going forward is coming back.
    • Qui non proficit, deficit – One who does not advance, loses ground.
    • Sedit qui timuit ne non succederet – "He who feared he would not succeed sat still" (Horace). For fear of failure he did nothing.

    Principle 15 Inverted. Static State

    • Semper idem – "Always the same thing" (Cicero).
    • Quieta non movere – Do not move settled things. Do not rock the boat.
    • Si fractum non sit, noli id reficere – If it isn't broken, don't fix it.
    • In dubiis non est agendum – In dubious cases, you should not act.
    • Verba volant, scripta manent – Words fly away, writings remain.
    • Dulce est desipere in loco – "It is sweet to relax at the proper time" (Horace).

    Principle 16. Partial or Excessive Action

    A. If 100 percent of object or system is hard to achieve using a given solution method then, by using 'slightly less' or 'slightly more' of the same method, the problem may be considerably easier to solve.

    • Impossibilium nulla obligatio est – "Nobody has any obligation to the impossible" (Corpus Iuris Civilis).
    • Boni pastoris est tondere pecus, non deglubere – "A good shepherd shears his sheep, he doesn't flay them" (Tiberius). Don't tax the populace excessively.
    • Exigo ame non ut optimis par sim, sed ut malis melior – "I require myself not to be equal to the best, but to be better than the bad" (Seneca).
    • Nec verbum verbo curabis reddere fidus interpres – "As a true translator you will take care not to translate word for word" (Horace).
    • Cum grano salis – "With a grain of salt" (Pliny the Elder). With some allowance or modification. Take something not literally, but with due consideration.
    • Ne quid nimis – "Not too much" (Terence). Nothing in excess.

    Principle 16 Inverted. Extremism (All or Nothing)

    • Per fas et nefas – With right and wrong. By any means necessary.
    • Nunc aut numquam – Now or never.
    • Aut Caesar aut nullus – "Either Caesar or nobody" (Cesare Borgia). Either in first place or nowhere.
    • Aut vincere aut mori – Either conquer or die. Death or victory.
    • Tertium non datur – No third is given. Logical axiom that a claim is either true or false, with no third option.
    • De mortuis nihil nisi bene – "Of the dead, nothing but good" (Chilon). Say only good things about the dead.
    • Nil actum reputa si quid superest agendum – "Nothing has been done if anything is undone" (Lucan).
    • Aut disce aut discede – Either learn or leave.
    • Fiat iustitia pereat mundus – "Let justice be done, though the world should perish" (Ferdinand I).
    • Fiat iustitia, ruat caelum – Let justice be done, though the heavens fall.
    • Lex malla, lex nulla – "A bad law is no law" (St. Thomas Aquinas).
    • Ubi ius incertum, ibi ius nullum – Where the law is uncertain, there is no law.
    • Meos tam suspicione quam crimine iudico carere oportere – "I feel that members of my family should never be suspected of breaking the law" (Caesar).
    • Quia suam uxorem etiam suspiciore vacare vellet – "Caesar's wife may not be suspected" (Plutarch)
    • Nil actum reputa si quid superest agendum – "Don't consider that anything has been done if anything is left to be done" (Lucan).

    Principle 17. Another Dimension or Scale

    A. If object or system contains or moves in a straight line, consider use of dimensions or movement outside the line. Move object or system in two-dimensional space.
    B. If object or system contains or moves in a plane, consider use of dimensions or movement outside the current plane. Move object or system in three-dimensional space.
    C. Use a multi-story arrangement of objects or systems instead of a single-story arrangement.

    • In virtute sunt multi ascensus – "There are many degrees in excellence" (Cicero).

    D. Tilt or re-orient object or system, lay it on its side. Look from another angle.

    • Maior e longinquo reverentia – "Viewed from a distance, everything is beautiful" (Tacitus).
    • Sub specie aeternitatis – "From eternity's point of view" (Spinoza).

    E. Use 'another side' of a given area. Look at reverse side of a medal.

    • Nemo autem regere potest nisi qui et regi – "Moreover, there is no one who can rule unless he can be ruled" (Seneca).
    • Necesse est multos timeat quem multi timent – "He must fear many, whom many fear" (Laberius referring to Caesar).
    • Accipere quam facere praestat injuriam – It is better to suffer an injustice than to do an injustice.
    • Bono vinci satius est quam almo more iniuriam vincere – "A good man would rather suffer defeat than defeat another by foul means" (Sallust).
    • Si vis amaria, ama – "If you want to be loved, love" (Seneca).
    • Ut ameris, amabilis esto – Be amiable, then you'll be loved.

    F. Use another scale.

    • De minimis non curat praetor (rex, lex) – The authority (king, law) does not care about trivial things.
    • Aquila non captat muscas – The eagle doesn't capture flies. Don't be concerned of the small things.
    • Elephantum ex musca facis – To make elephant from a fly.
    • Parturient montes, nascetur ridiculus mus – "Mountains will be in labour, and a ridiculous mouse will be born" (Horace). Much ado about nothing.

    B. Increase its frequency (even up to the ultrasonic).
    C. Use object or system resonant frequency.

    A. Instead of continuous action, use periodic or pulsating actions.
    B. If an action is already periodic, change the periodic magnitude or frequency.

    • Consuetudo altera natura est – Habit is second nature.

    • Non est vivere sed valere vita est – "Life is not being alive but being well" (Martial).
    • Non uno die Roma aedificata est – Rome was not built in one day.
    • Ceterum censeo Carthaginem esse delendam – "Therefore, I conclude that Carthage must be destroyed" (Cato the Elder). Cato used to end every speech to the Senate, on any subject whatsoever, with this phrase.

    Principle 21. Skipping (Quickness, Decisiveness)

    Principle 26. Copying

    Principle 35. Parameter or Property Change

    A. Change object or system physical state (e.g., to a gas, liquid, or solid).
    B. Change the concentration or consistency.
    C. Change the degree of flexibility.
    D. Change the temperature.
    E. Change the pressure.
    F. Change other parameters.

    • Cibi condimentum est fames – Hunger is a spice for any meal.
    • Flecti, non frangi – To be bent, not broken.
    • In vino veritas – The truth is in wine. A drunk person tells the truth.
    • Bonum vinum laetificat cor hominis – Good wine gladdens a human heart.
    • Nemo saltat sobrius – No man dances sober.
    • Post cenam non stare sed mille passus meare – Do not rest after dinner, but walk a mile.

    Principle 36. Phase Transition (Life Metamorphoses)

    A. Use phenomena occurring during phase transition (e.g., volume changes, loss or absorption of heat, etc.).

    • Memento mori – Remember, you will die.
    • Respice post te, mortalem te esse memento – "Look around you, remember that you are mortal" (Tertullian).
    • Hodie mihi, cras tibi – "It is my lot today, yours to-morrow" (Old epitaphs).
    • Stat sua cuique dies – "The date is set for each and everyone" (Virgil).
    • Mors ultima linea rerum est – "Death is everything's final limit" (Horace).
    • Pulvis et umbra sumus – "We are but dust and shadow" (Horace).
    • Sic transit gloria mundi – "Thus perishes the glory of the world" (Thomas A.Kempis).
    • Nascentes morimur – From the moment we are born, we begin to die.
    • Nemo ante mortem beatus – Nobody is blessed before his death.
    • Cineri gloria sera venit – Fame comes too late to the dead.
    • Extinctus amabitur idem – "The same man will be loved when he is dead" (Horace).
    • Omnia mutantur; nihil interit – "All things are changed; nothing dies" (Ovid).
    • Bis vivit qui bene vivit – He lives twice who lives well.
    • Cras amet qui nunquam amavit – May he love tomorrow who has never loved before.
    • Ars longa, vita brevis – "Art is long, but life is short" (The Latin translation by Horace of a phrase from Hippocrates).
    • Inter arma enim silent Musae – "During wars arts are silent" (Cicero).
    • Donec eris felix, multos numerabis amicos; tempora si fuerint nubila, solus eris – "As long as you are lucky, you will have many friends; if cloudy times appear, you will be alone" (Ovid).
    • Ad praesens ova cras pullis sunt meliora – Eggs today are better than chickens tomorrow.

    Principle 37. Thermal Expansion (Fervor, Passion)

    A. Use thermal expansion (or contraction) of materials.
    B. If thermal expansion is being used, use multiple materials with different coefficients of thermal expansion.

    • Igne natura renovatur integra (INRI) – "Through fire nature is reborn whole" (An alchemical aphorism).
    • Igni et ferro – By fire and sward.
    • Parva scintilla saepe magnam flamam excitat – A small spark often initiates a large flame.
    • In flagrante delicto – In flaming crime. Caught red-handed.
    • Pro aris et focis – "For altar and hearth" (Cicero). For our homes.
    • Ab imo pectore – "From the depths of my chest" (Attributed to Julius Caesar). From my heart.
    • Ex abundancia cordis, os loquitor – From the abundance of the heart the mouth speaks.
    • Amor ordinem nescit – "Love does not know order" (St. Jerome).
    • Amantes sunt amentes – Lovers are lunatics.
    • Amare et sapere vix deo conceditur – Even a god finds it hard to love and be wise at the same time.
    • Omnia vincit amor, nos et cedamus amori – "Love conquers all, let us too yield to love" (Virgil).
    • Gaudeamus igitur iuvenes dum sumus – "Thus let us enjoy ourselves as long as we are young" (From an old German student's song).

    Principle 38. Strong Oxidants (Inspiration, Enthusiasm)

    A. Replace common air with oxygen-enriched air.
    B. Replace enriched air with pure oxygen.
    C. Expose air or oxygen to ionizing radiation.
    D. Use ionized oxygen.
    E. Replace ozonized (or ionized) oxygen with ozone.
    F. Insert an active ingredient.

    • Ad maiorem dei gloriam (AMDG) – For the greater glory of God.
    • Ave Caesar! Morituri te salutamus – "Hail Caesar! We who are about to die salute you" (Gladiators before the fight).
    • Cogito, ergo sum – "I think; therefore I am" (Rene Descartes).
    • Dum spiro, spero – "As long as I breathe, I hope" (Cicero).
    • Credula vitam spes fovet et melius cras fore semper dicit – "Credulous hope supports our life, and always says that tomorrow will be better" (Tibullus).
    • Auribus tenere lupum – "I hold a wolf by the ears" (Terence). I am in a dangerous situation and dare not let go.
    • Nil desperandum! – "Never despair!" (Horace).
    • Luctor et emergo – I struggle but I will survive.
    • Ne cede malis, seal contra audentior ito – Do not yield to misfortunes, on the contrary, go more boldly to meet them.
    • In vinculis etiam audax – In chains yet still bold (free).
    • Ad hominem – Appealing to feelings rather than reason.
    • Animus facit nobilem – The spirit makes (human) noble.
    • Crede quod habes, et habes – Believe that you have it, and you have it.
    • In magnis et voluisse sat est – "To once have wanted is enough in great deeds" (Propertius).
    • Nisi credideritis, non intelligetis – "Unless you will have believed, you will not understand" (St. Augustine).
    • Trahimur omnes laudis studio – "We are all drawn by our eagerness for praise" (Cicero).

    Principle 39. Inert Environment (Neutrality, Indifference)

    A. Replace a normal environment with an inert one.
    B. Add neutral parts, or inert additives to object or system.

    • Nec habeo, nec careo, nec curo – Not have, not lack, not care.
    • Sine ira et studio – "Without anger or bias" (Tacitus).
    • Audi, vide, tace, si tu vis vivere – Hear, see, be silent, if you wish to be alive.
    • Bene qui latuit, bene vixit – "He who lives unnoticed lives well" (Ovid).
    • Qui tacet, consentire videtur – Who is silent seems to agree.
    • Favete linguis – "Keep quiet" (Horace).
    • Si tacuisses, philosophus mansisses – "If you had kept your silence, you would have stayed a philosopher" (Boethius).
    • Nil admirari – "To not admire anything" (Horace). You shouldn't let yourself be taken away by anything.
    • Aequam memento rebus in arduis servare mentem – "In adversity, remember to keep an even mind" (Horace).
    • Cuivis dolori remedium est patientia – Patience is the cure for all suffering.
    • Gloriosum est iniurias oblivisci – It is glorious to forget injustice.
    • Qui dormit non peccet – He who sleeps does not sin.
    • Pax melior est quam iustissimum bellum – Peace is better than the most just war.
    • Bella gerant alii – Let others wage war.
    • Vix ulla tam iniqua pax, quin bello vel aequissimo sit potior – "Scarcely is there any peace so unjust that it is not better than even the fairest war" (Erasmus).

    Principle 40. Composite Structures ("Renaissance People")

    A. Change from uniform to composite (multiple) materials.

    • Mens sana in corpore sano – "A sound mind in a sound body" (Juvenal). Both physical and mental health are necessary.
    • Omne tulit punctum qui miscuit utile dulci – "He has gained every point who has combined the useful and the agreeable" (Orazio).
    • Rara mistura cum sapientia forma – The combination of beauty and wisdom is infrequent.
    • Suaviter in modo, fortiter in re – Gentle in manner, resolute in deed.
    • Panem et circenses – "Bread and circus games" (Juvenal). Food and games to keep people happy.
    • Inventas vitam iuvat excoluisse per artes – "Let us improve life through science and art" (Virgil).
